The Empathic Detective

Conflict in medical practices can arise instantly, especially at the front desk. When patients or colleagues lash out, do we listen like a Judge, looking for inaccuracies to win the argument, or like a Detective, seeking the underlying truth of their distress?

NARRATOR [0:00] At times we all do this. We listen, but do we really listen to understand? Or do we listen to find the mistake, to win the argument, to bring a guilty verdict? And honestly, it's totally understandable. When you're barraged with accusations and frustrations from 8 in the morning till half past 6 at night, it's natural to start listening like a judge... searching for the inaccuracy, building your case, building your defense. We've all done it. Just sit with that for a moment.

NARRATOR [0:34] What you're about to learn is how to move from being the judge to being the detective. How to find what's really going on with the patient when a situation turns conflictual. In 1980, psychologist George Miller proposed something radical: a different way of thinking. He said, before you disagree with someone, assume what they are saying is true. Then ask yourself: what could it be true of?

NARRATOR [1:15] This doesn't mean you have to agree with them, you just have to find the world in which their words make sense. Now, if that sounds soft to you, it isn't. This isn't about pretending people are right. It's about understanding why they believe what they believe, because until you know that, you cannot resolve anything. You're just two people shouting past each other. And if you're thinking, "what, so I just agree with everyone?", the answer is no. You don't have to agree with a single word, but if you skip straight to correcting someone, you will never find out what's actually driving their conversation. And that is the brilliance of Miller's Law: it's a tool, not a surrender. Let me show you what this looks like in practice.

PATIENT [2:15]

"I've been waiting here over an hour, and nobody cares about my health."

NARRATOR [2:21] You see the judge hears a lie: "12 minutes, not an hour as stated. Case closed!" And the judge responds with:

RECEPTIONIST (JUDGE) [2:33]

"Sir, it's only been 12 minutes."

NARRATOR [2:37] The detective, however, hears distress. If it feels like an hour to the patient, then they feel forgotten, dismissed, like they don't matter here. And that is the missing piece. The detective responds with:

RECEPTIONIST (DETECTIVE) [2:49]

"You are a priority to us, and I know that when you have to wait, it does not always feel that way. Let me check how long the doctor is going to be so you know exactly how long you are going to be waiting for."

NARRATOR [3:13] When you stop defending your version of events and start looking for the patient's, the argument dies. You stop fighting each other and start fighting the problem together. So next time something sounds wrong or incorrect, don't correct it. Ask yourself: What would have to be true for this to make sense?

The Judge Mindset

Listening to Defend & Correct

  • Focuses purely on factual inaccuracies, looking for structural errors in the patient's argument.
  • Preoccupied with building a defense, finding "proof" to render a guilty verdict.
  • Triggers defensiveness, escalating conflict immediately and alienating patients.
"Actually, our logs show you've only been waiting 12 minutes, not an hour."
The Detective Mindset

Listening to Understand Distress

  • Applies Miller's Law: assumes statements represent an internal reality, asking *what is this true of?*
  • Focuses on the emotional engine driving the behavior (fear, feeling forgotten, distress).
  • De-escalates instantly, turning the situation into collaborative problem-solving.
"You are a priority to us... Let me check how long the doctor is going to be."
Radical Psychology Principle
"To understand what another person is saying, you must assume that it is true, and try to find out what it could be true of."
George A. Miller (1980) Co-founder of Cognitive Psychology, Harvard University

Step-by-Step Conflict Escalation

Defense Reaction

GP Receptionist (Judge Mode) "Actually, you logged in exactly 12 minutes ago. We have several critically ill patients ahead of you."

Patient (Insulted / Escalated) "Are you calling me a liar? I know when I got here! You people are completely incompetent and lazy!"
Outcome: Trust is shattered. The patient feels invalidated. The receptionist is defensive and stressed. The timeline is stuck on defense of facts instead of resolution.

Step-by-Step Conflict Resolution

Empathy Search
P
Patient "I've been waiting here over an hour! This is absolute rubbish!"
GP Receptionist (Detective Mode) "You are a priority to us, and I know that when you have to wait, it does not always feel that way. Let me check how long the doctor is going to be so you know exactly how long you are going to be waiting for."
R
P
Patient (De-escalated / Softened) "Thank you. I have to catch a bus back to see my sick partner, and I was getting so panicked that I was going to miss it."
Outcome: The emotional "fire" is extinguished. The receptionist discovered the *actual* cause of the distress (the partner & the bus) rather than fighting over wait minutes. You can now work together to solve it.
